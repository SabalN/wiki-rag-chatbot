How do solar photovoltaic cells work?
Solar photovoltaic (PV) cells convert sunlight directly into electricity using the photoelectric effect. Here's how the process works:

1. Photon absorption — Sunlight hits a semiconductor (usually silicon), and photons with sufficient energy excite electrons, knocking them loose from their atoms.

2. p-n junction — The cell is built with two layers: a p-type (positive) and n-type (negative) silicon layer. This creates a built-in electric field at the junction.

3. Electron flow — The electric field pushes freed electrons toward the n-type layer and holes toward the p-type layer, creating a directional current — that's usable electricity.

Typical commercial silicon cells convert 15–22% of incident sunlight into electricity. Multi-junction cells used in satellites can exceed 40% efficiency.

Solar photovoltaic cells generate electricity through the
photoelectric effect. When photons from sunlight strike a
silicon semiconductor, they transfer energy to electrons,
exciting them into the conduction band.
 
The cell's p-n junction — formed by doping silicon with
boron (p-type) and phosphorus (n-type) — creates an internal
electric field. This field separates the excited electrons
from their corresponding holes, driving them through an
external circuit as usable direct current (DC).
 
Commercial silicon panels typically achieve 15–22% efficiency.
Solar PV costs have dropped ~90% since 2010, making it the
cheapest electricity source in history in many markets.
